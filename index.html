Chapter One

The First Day of School

The castle always felt familiar.

Jenna stood at the edge of its stone balcony, her palms resting against granite worn smooth by centuries of wind and weather. Below her, the land unfolded in endless waves of green — forests breathing slowly in the distance, a thin silver river threading through the valley like a memory refusing to fade.

Above her stretched an impossibly wide sky.

"Jenna…"

The voice drifted through the wind.

Not loud. Not urgent. But familiar enough to make her chest tighten.

She turned slowly.

Behind her stretched a dim corridor lit by trembling torchlight. Shadows crawled along ancient stone walls. Something about this place felt less like a dream and more like a forgotten memory — as if she had once stood here before and left something unfinished.

"Jenna."

Closer now.

She began walking toward the heavy wooden door at the end of the corridor. Her footsteps echoed softly beneath the vaulted ceiling. When she finally reached the handle, she hesitated.

The iron was cold.

Colder than it should have been.

She twisted it slowly.

The door groaned open.

And the voice vanished.
Silence rushed in like a held breath.

She stepped forward — then turned too quickly.

Her head struck the wall with a dull crack.

—

Jenna woke with a sharp inhale.

The castle ceiling dissolved into the familiar white of her bedroom. Morning light spilled lazily through the curtains, warming the floor in uneven stripes.

She pressed her palm against her forehead and groaned.

"Another dream… Why can't my life take a break for once?"

"Jenna!" her mother called from downstairs.

The voice felt strangely similar to the one from the dream — only warmer, grounded in reality.

Her eyes drifted to the clock.

9:30.

Her stomach dropped.

First day of high school.

"Mom! Why didn't you wake me up earlier?" she shouted, scrambling out of bed.

"You said five more minutes," her mother called back calmly.

Jenna rushed through her morning like a storm — water splashing, drawers opening and closing, a towel dragged hastily across damp hair.

She pulled on her favorite outfit without much thought: a black top embroidered with subtle floral patterns that traced down to her waist, paired with smoky grey pants that moved easily when she walked.

It wasn't perfect.

But it felt like her.

In the kitchen, a plate waited on the table — pancakes soaked in maple syrup and crowned with a ridiculous amount of whipped cream.

Her mother believed in sending people into the world with something sweet.

Jenna grabbed a fork and took a large bite.

"Did you brush your teeth?" her mother asked.

Jenna froze.

She glanced at the clock.

9:48.

Dropping the fork, she sprinted down the hallway and returned moments later, grabbing the pancake with both hands and squeezing it slightly as whipped cream slipped from the sides.

"Love you!" she mumbled through a mouthful as she rushed out the door.

The school bus screeched to a halt outside.

The doors folded open.

"Good morning, young lady," the driver greeted warmly.

Jenna smiled quickly and stepped inside, too rushed to take in the bus fully. She walked straight toward the back where a few familiar faces from middle school waved her over.

Within seconds she was talking animatedly.

"Did you guys see the game last night? The way Stephen Curry hit those three-pointers — it wasn't even shooting, it was like performance art. Every shot looked inevitable. Like the ball already knew where it was going."

Her voice carried easily across the bus.

Near the front, a boy named Jade half-listened to his friend enthusiastically describing a recent trip to Hawaii.

But Jenna's voice drifted forward.

Jade turned slightly in his seat.

She was laughing, hands moving as she spoke. Her hair looked faintly unsettled, as if she had rushed out without bothering to fix it properly. Her shirt rested slightly uneven on her shoulder. There was a faint trace of whipped cream near the corner of her lip.

It wasn't the kind of thing most people would notice.
But Jade did.

Not because it was polished.
Because it wasn't.

"Who's the girl in the back?" Jade asked his friend.

"That's Jenna," his friend replied casually. "You don't remember her? We were in the same elementary school."

Jade searched his memory.

A vague, blurred image surfaced — a quieter girl, indistinct. Nothing like the person behind him now.

He tried to reconcile the faded memory with the girl currently dissecting a basketball game like it was poetry.

The bus screeched to a halt as it pulled into the school parking lot.

Students began standing and filing out.

Jenna stepped off the bus — then paused.

Buzz. Buzz.

Her phone.

She sighed and climbed back inside to grab it from the seat.

As she reached for it, she noticed someone watching.

Jade.

Up close he looked composed — hair neatly combed, shirt perfectly ironed, a tie hanging straight down his collar as if he had carefully prepared for the day.

"I don't think phones are allowed for students," he said.

Jenna slipped the phone into her pocket and smirked.

"That's only a problem if they find out."

And just like that, she stepped off the bus again and disappeared into the morning crowd.

Jade remained seated for a moment longer.

Something about her unsettled him.

Not because she was loud.

But because she seemed untouched by the quiet calculations he had already begun making about this new chapter.

He adjusted his tie unconsciously.

And for the first time that morning,
he felt slightly unprepared.

Chapter Two

The Fated Meetings

Students shuffled into their seats while quiet conversations filled the room. Chairs scraped against the floor, backpacks slid beneath desks, and the low hum of first-week uncertainty hung in the air.

A small, quiet relief passed through him.

She was in the same class.

Chapter Three

The Goddess at the Window

The classroom felt unusually quiet that afternoon.

A few desks remained empty.

One of them was Jenna's.

Jade noticed it almost immediately. At first, he tried to ignore it. Students around him whispered about homework, upcoming assignments, and the usual trivial things that filled a school day. He leaned slightly forward in his seat, pretending to read while listening carefully to the scattered conversations around him.

Maybe someone would mention where she was.

He tried to overhear something — anything.

But nothing came.

No one seemed particularly concerned.

A faint restlessness settled inside him.

Finally, one student casually mentioned that Jenna had gone to the infirmary earlier because she wasn't feeling well. That was all.

The teacher continued the lesson, but Jade found it harder to concentrate. His thoughts drifted again and again toward the empty desk.

After class ended, he stepped outside.

The hallway was quiet now, washed in the warm gold of late afternoon sunlight.

And that was when he saw her.

Jenna stood near a large window at the end of the corridor.

For a moment, Jade stopped walking.

Everything else faded.

Students passing by,
distant conversations,
the echo of footsteps —

all of it blurred into the background.

She became the only thing in focus.

A gentle breeze slipped through the half-open window, brushing against her hair. Strands lifted softly and separated, drifting around her face like delicate threads of light, as if the quiet secrets of the world had chosen that moment to reveal themselves.

The sunlight touched her gently, reflecting in her eyes and along the edges of her hair, giving them a soft glow. Her hair seemed to twinkle like stars scattered across a calm night sky, each tiny strand catching the light in fleeting, perfect sparks. To Jade, her eyes looked almost luminous — like a golden star that had been quietly hiding there all along.

Without realizing it, his gaze memorized every small detail of her face: the gentle curve of her brow, the soft bloom of sunlight along her nose, the subtle lift of her lips that hinted at countless untold stories.

Everything about her felt impossibly precise.

Impossibly perfect.

And before he could stop himself, a word escaped his mouth.

"Beautiful."

The word was soft. Almost a whisper.

Jenna turned her head slightly, searching the hallway.

She didn't call out. She didn't notice him.

Jade's heart jumped violently in his chest.

He stepped back quickly, disappearing behind the wall beside the corridor.

A sudden wave of panic rushed through him. His throat tightened, choking off any sound that might betray him. It felt as though the air itself had grown thick, leaving no space for words.

For a few seconds, he simply stood there, pressed against the wall, breathing quietly and hoping she wouldn't notice him.

A moment later, footsteps approached Jenna. One of her friends walked up beside her.

"What happened to you? I heard you went to the infirmary."

Jenna shrugged lightly.

"It was just a stomach ache," she said, brushing it off with an easy smile.

Then she pushed herself away from the window.

"Come on. Let's go back to class, shall we?"

They walked down the hallway together.

Jade remained where he was for another moment, staring at the empty space where she had been standing.

The sunlight still rested on the windowsill.

But somehow
the hallway felt dimmer now.

Chapter Four

Twilight Conversations

Jenna returned home the way she often did — slightly unruly, slightly distracted, and completely herself.

She tossed her bag onto the couch and headed straight for the television.

Within seconds, the screen filled with basketball highlights.

Her eyes lit up.

Every crossover, every pass, every impossible three-pointer replayed in dramatic slow motion. To Jenna, it wasn't just a sport — it was choreography, rhythm, instinct. She leaned forward, absorbed as if she were witnessing something extraordinary.

Then suddenly —

Snap.

The screen went black.

The power had gone out.

Jenna groaned loudly.

"Seriously?"

But the frustration lasted only a moment. She stretched her arms lazily and sighed, remembering something else she had meant to do.

"The plants."

She walked back inside and retrieved her favorite watering can — an old metal one painted a soft green that had faded slightly with time.

Stepping into the small garden behind her house, she moved from plant to plant, watering each carefully.

"How are you doing today?" she asked one softly, tilting her head as if waiting for an answer.

"And you," she continued with another, "is the wind taking good care of you?"

The leaves rustled faintly in the evening breeze.

Jenna smiled.

After finishing with the last plant, she straightened up and waved lightly toward them.

"Goodbye, everyone."

Then she climbed the ladder leading to the terrace.

The evening air was cooler there.

She stood quietly, her head tilted upward, gazing into the open sky. The horizon stretched endlessly, where the deep blue above seemed to melt into the earth below. For a moment, it felt as if she were inside a perfect globe, and the curve of the earth beneath her eyes revealed how vast and round the world truly was.

She often wondered how people had once believed the earth was flat. The sky was far too immense for that.

Jenna wandered slowly across the terrace, watching the faint shapes drifting across her vision — the little floaters that danced across her sight whenever she stared too long at the open sky.

Then something caught her attention.

Movement.

A dark shape spread across the sky.

At first it was only a few.

Then dozens.

Then hundreds.

Soon, the sky seemed alive with them.

A vast herd of bats swept across the twilight, their wings slicing silently through the air as they began their evening emergence flight. To Jenna, it looked as if there were thousands of them, moving together like a living cloud that twisted and turned with the fading light.

She watched in quiet awe.

The spectacle felt almost magical.

For a brief moment, a childish thought crossed her mind — one she had carried since she was younger.

What if one of them suddenly swooped down and bit her?

Would she turn into a vampire?

She imagined sharp canines and pale moonlit nights, and laughed softly to herself. Some childhood fantasies refused to fade.

Gradually, the bats disappeared into the deepening sky, their dark shapes dissolving into the distance until nothing remained but the quiet evening.

The brilliant blue above slowly softened.

The sky turned orange.

Then grey.

As twilight began to steal the heat away, Jenna looked toward the horizon where the sun hovered for its final moments. She folded her arms loosely and whispered softly,

"At the end, it's you and me…
and so at the beginning."

A small smile touched her lips.

"Thank you for showing up every day."

The sun slipped away.

The sky dimmed.

And after one last glance upward,
Jenna turned and climbed back down
into the house.

It wasn't there.

The thin chain that usually held the small pendant around her neck was gone.

"No… no…"

But it didn't.

Her heart began to race.

That pendant wasn't just jewelry.

Her mother had given it to her years ago — on a day Jenna remembered clearly. Since then, she had worn it almost every day.

It felt like a small piece of home
that she carried with her wherever she went.

Nothing.

Swish.

Jade Melbourne

favorite players list

Jade.
Her Jade.

I never properly thanked him for the pendant.

Let's meet at the restaurant near school at 3 pm.

He read it again. And again.

His ears felt warm.

"Is this… a date?" he whispered to himself.

What am I supposed to do?

What should I wear?

What if I say something stupid?

For someone who handled exams, sports competitions, and difficult challenges without hesitation… this felt strangely terrifying.

At exactly three in the afternoon, Jade arrived at the restaurant. He walked inside cautiously and scanned the room. Then he spotted Jenna. And stopped.

Jade slowly walked over and sat across from her.

Jenna looked up happily, a bit of cream on her cheek and the corner of her lips. She didn't seem to notice.

Jade couldn't help it.

"You should try the cake here," she said enthusiastically. "It's really good."

"I can see that."

At one point, Jenna gently touched the pendant around her neck.

"By the way… I never properly thanked you for finding this," she said softly.

"It's really important to me."

Jade shook his head.

"It was nothing."

But he remembered how relieved she had looked that day. So it didn't feel like nothing to him.

Jenna noticed Jade in ways she hadn't before. There was something about the way he carried himself — quiet, steady, untouched by the restlessness that seemed to follow everyone else.

When he looked at her, it wasn't the careless glance most people gave. His gaze lingered with a quiet sincerity, filled with a compassion that didn't demand anything in return. It felt as if he wasn't simply seeing her, but trying to understand her — patiently, gently.

There was a strange comfort in it. A warmth that didn't press too close but stayed present, like sunlight resting on a quiet afternoon.

His movements were unhurried, almost thoughtful, as though every gesture belonged exactly where it was meant to be.

In that moment, Jenna realized something
she hadn't been able to put into words before.

Jade didn't feel like someone
trying to impress the world.

He felt complete within it.

And watching him there, calm and unshaken, she couldn't help but think that he looked less like someone simply standing in the moment — and more like a piece of art placed carefully within it.

Eventually, they finished their food and stepped outside together. The evening air felt calm. They walked slowly down the quiet street.

After a moment, Jenna suddenly asked,

"What do you think true love is?"

Jade blinked, slightly surprised by the question. He thought for a moment before answering.

"Maybe… it's giving everything you can for the other person."

Then he looked at her.

"What do you think it is?"

Jenna looked ahead for a moment. Then she turned toward him, her voice steady and sudden:

"But… do you want to find out with me?"

Her eyes met his, searching, hopeful — and then, without pause, she added,

"I love you, Jade."

Time seemed to stop. Jade's heart thundered in his chest. The air between them thickened. The quiet street, the dim evening light, even the faint hum of distant traffic — all faded into nothing.

For a brief instant, he couldn't believe what he had just heard.

Then tears rushed unexpectedly to his eyes —
sudden and unguarded —

as if those three simple words
had reached a place within him
that had been waiting far longer
than he had realized.

Then he reached out, placing his hands gently on her shoulders. He searched her eyes, seeing every flicker of courage and vulnerability reflected back.

"I love you."

He said it calm but deep, a certainty he hadn't realized he could feel.

He leaned slightly closer, almost imperceptibly, enough that the warmth of his presence enveloped her.

"If there's ever something you feel like you can't do… or something that scares you. You don't have to carry it alone."

The evening air moved gently around them as they walked. Jade's hands stayed steady, relaxed at his sides, but his presence itself felt grounding.

"If the path ahead ever feels uncertain… or if you find yourself hesitating because you're not sure what to do — then rely on me."

Another small pause followed, natural and unforced.

"Trust me with the things you're unsure about. I might not always have the perfect answer — but I'll stand with you while you figure it out."

"So… trust me."

Jenna's lips curved into a soft smile.

Although she was usually cheerful and outgoing, there were quiet moments when she felt strangely empty when she was alone. But somehow, when she was with him, that feeling disappeared without effort.

And as they walked side by side,
just the two of them,

the world didn't need to
make perfect sense.

It was enough
that they had found each other.

Later that evening, Jenna's father observed her quietly from the living room. She was sitting on the couch, smiling faintly while messaging someone on her phone.

The smile was small, but it carried a warmth he hadn't seen before.

Subtle.

Meaningful.

Something important
had just begun in her life.

Chapter Seven

The Day of Christmas Eve

Christmas Eve had wrapped the entire city in warmth and light.

The streets were filled with people strolling along the sidewalks, admiring brightly decorated shop windows and Christmas displays. Red ribbons, glowing lights, and festive music filled the air.

Families walked together, stopping at restaurants to enjoy dinner and celebrate the evening.

Jade was walking down the footpath, quietly observing the cheerful atmosphere around him.

Then he noticed someone familiar.

A few steps ahead, Jenna was walking with her parents and her younger brother.

They were laughing together as they admired the decorated shops.

Jade slowed down.

He had often wondered what Jenna's parents were like, and part of him had hoped that someday he would meet them.

Maybe this is the perfect chance.

But then another thought crossed his mind.

I shouldn't interrupt their family time.

He was about to continue walking when a voice called out from across the path.

"Jade!"

He turned.

Jenna had spotted him.

She waved enthusiastically.

"Come here!"

Jade hesitated for a moment before crossing the street and walking toward them.

Jenna looked almost excited.

"Mom, Dad — this is Jade," she said happily.

"He's the one who found my pendant."

Her parents exchanged surprised glances.

"Oh my," her mother said warmly. "Is that so?"

Mr. Trace smiled.

"Well then, we definitely can't let him go empty-handed tonight."

Jade felt slightly embarrassed.

"It was really nothing," he said politely. "I'm sorry if I interrupted your evening. I should probably leave now."

He turned slightly, about to walk away.

But Mr. Trace stopped him.

"You can join us for dinner if you'd like."

He glanced at Jenna.

"Isn't that right?"

Jenna smiled and nodded.

"But I don't want to take away your family time," Jade replied respectfully.

Mr. Trace chuckled.

"Haha, I insist. Don't worry about that."

So the group walked together to a nearby restaurant.

They sat down at a comfortable table, and soon food was ordered.

Mr. Trace leaned slightly toward Jade.

"So, Jade… how are classes going these days?"

"Good, sir," Jade replied carefully.
